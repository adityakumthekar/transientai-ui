The 2 day first week of Jan behind us…..then the week of saying happy new year behind us….we now find ourselves truly back to school and getting into the swing of things.

 

Long one to start the year……

 

It has already been a choppy start to 2025. A lot of the ‘euphoria’ and animal spirits that came with the Trump victory, the Bessent announcement and a broader view of risk on backstopped by de-regulation, efficiency, Ai and US supremacy now faltering (if only a touch). The US market reversal last week a nod to the view that ‘where Nvidia goes the market follows’….with the US heavyweight now down ~10% from it’s Jan 6th all time high. In fact Global equities have been sold 3 of the last 4 weeks and the majority of that selling has been driven by a growth in the short book. A combination of growing anxiety, absolute market levels, high gross length and yields proving enough to warrant some  proving discipline.

 

For me….Inflation/re-inflation and yields are the real ‘ball game’. Non farm payrolls last week was a shock to the system and the big beat (+265k) enough to drive a yields up, $ up and equities down rotation. US 10 year and 30 year have now had a material rise and the 10 year in particular within touching distance of the April 24 highs and the key psychological level of 5%. The US is not the only region to be dealing with bond market worries…..the UK now seeing it’s own 10 year hitting levels not seen since 2008 and GBP suffering from an emerging market like bout of volatility and de-risking. All of this at a time where Oil is one of the best performing assets ytd…the tragic LA fires likely driving other forms of inflation (rebuild/insurance) and Trump 2.0 just around the corner.

 

As a result……I’d break this week into two halves…the first half heavy as we wait for CPI and today will determine what comes next. If we see another hot number the market is vulnerable…a weaker number and the inflation/re-inflation genie might calm…..and if supported by strong TSMC numbers and a good start to earnings (banks)….that might be enough to see the ‘old trade’ reassert itself. (see table below for implied move expectations depending on number).

 

 

PUBLIC;

2025. As we look forward into 2025….what will be the themes. The continued growth of Private assets as a segment with Private Credit moving from welterweight to heavyweight and PE having growth from a $1 trillion aum industry to over $12 trillion in a short period of time. The Narrowness of the equity market….everywhere…..with 5 US stocks accounting for ~20% of the global equity market cap….50% of the S&P returns in 2024…and increasingly only a handful of stocks (Mag 7 or Granolas) accounting for a higher and higher % of benchmark indexes globally. US exceptionalism playing out once again in terms of market performance…in terms of economy….in terms of investor focus. The pressure on exits and DPI growing with the IPO markets not open for all assets…or at least not open at a price that sellers will accept. . Trump 2.0…time will tell what this will bring in terms of headline risks and sounbite volatility…..but the tightrope of spending/deficit to be offset by growth/efficiency while landing the inflation/rates normalisation journey….is no small feat. I suspect de-regulation will the opening gambits for the new administration and time will tell whether that drives a real shift in corporate attitudes towards the cost/benefit of going after M&A. I imagine any attempt at prediction is foolhardy…however…..i would imagine some of the following being in focus to a greater or lesser extend. Continued focus on all things MENA – in terms of issuance and growing market eco system, in terms of dedicated capital allocated to region and in terms of flight on human capital and office openings and investors and talent continue to allocate more resources to the region. I imagine (hope) there has to be some European response to the structural (and increasingly existential) issues they face….a block of ~300m but with too much regulation, too many handbrakes, too little growth and too much dependence on other regions (who are either out of favour or about to tariff). M&A…with less GDP growth and better regulatory framework I suspect we will see much more activity in the US and from the US. I also think we are starting to see green shoots of capitulation in Europe around the need for intra-consolidation and eventually cross border consolidation. More value unlocks via spin offs, division sales, re-lists, PE sales and/or capital return (increasingly buybacks over divs)…at the end of the day if others wont buy your shares because stock is too small or a region out of focus….then companies can/should. As a final thought….I think the 3 D’s are going to be in focus…not just next year but as themes that will impact the coming years…..de-equitisation (private over public and private for longer), de-regulation (watch the first 100 days for Trump/Bessent) and De-globalisation (Tariffs/Trade wars).

 

Indexation and correlation. Some of these moves make me reflect on something I mentioned a few weeks ago….namely indexation and correlation and what happens when the trend shifts. “The market make up has changed dramatically over the past few years. The nature of investors shifting more and more to passive and etf. The hedge fund industry increasingly to market neutral. The emergence of systematic quant where price and price momentum is as important as fundamentals and valuation. These shifts have likely resulted in a few second order impacts….the quantum of single stock moves around earnings surprises (driven by stops)….a shrinking of duration from the overall market…but perhaps the biggest impact of all is the growth of momentum as a factor. What do I mean by this? Well in a market where the S&P is increasingly narrow and driven by 5 stocks (and more recently 2)…..and where over 60% of the ytd returns have been driven by 6 stocks (35% by 1 stock) then we live in a market eco system where an ever increasing % of aum is sat in index tracking or index hugging wrappers/funds…and the higher things go…the more people ‘have’ to buy them….which mean the higher they go. This community is never a seller while these stocks go up…as they have no view on fair value and whether something has overrun. These ‘winners’ in turn brings retail to the fore…and they tend to be more interesting in trend and momentum and assets that have ‘performed’ and as such are more likely to by FAANG, MAG7 or Nvidia than to take a bet on a cheap but underperforming value name. this in part is fuel that drives like Nvidia from $1 trillion to $2 trillion to $3.3 trillion. Of course a huge amount of that appreciation if fundamental and deserved but a huge amount of it is also technical and a new dynamic to the markets.”

 

Market evolution. A topic of interest to me more and more. We saw Clif Asness discuss it in a recent ‘less efficient market’ thought piece. We’ve seen European companies respond to it via de-listing and listing choices. We’ve seen it play out through volatility and dislocation at moments. If asset allocators look at the market like a widget and base their interactions with it on purely fundamental factors then increasingly left feeling confused. Much of what drives sentiment, gross and flows is built around a variety of inputs that have little to do with fundamentals. Factors, correlation, vol, risk, var etc. but how robust are the frameworks on which these assessments built? We saw strange correlations appear around the time of the yen carry unwind, we continue to see savage basis/positioning unwinds on concentrated positioning. Many of these models have been built over the last 10 years....a period of low (relative to history) vol (ex covid), a one way central bank policy of liquidity provision, a response to ‘danger’ of Fed puts and stimulus, a buy the dip or stay calm and carry on public market mindset (retail and insto)…but how much of this will hold in the future? If we start to see politicisation of central banks? If we see the social contract between public and politicians fracture, if we see mainstays such as $ being the reserve currency start to unwind….and if we see an acceleration of de-globalisation play out. If your performance is only as good your model how well can ones model factor the future?

 

UK. In a world where capital will flow where it is welcome and rewarded…and IP (human and corporate) will move to where it is rational and incentivised….the UK is finding itself in a tricky spot. A client said the first budget focussed on ‘ideology’ and ‘settling scores’ and in doing so forgot to think about ‘growth’ and ‘incentives’….and that is before we talk about the weather or London traffic. The markets missed a beat but have acted with brutal haste in 2025 to give their verdict on this strategy. It will be interesting to see what Reeves/Starmer do next….what is clear…is that hope is not a strategy and something needs to be done.

 

Trump 2.0. Not going to delve into too much detail here….but…..last week saw press conferences, headlines, Greenland invasion talk, Tariff talk,’Gulf of America’, Panama, Denmark tariffs…and a reminder that with Trump comes headline risk and a need to remember the 2016 Trump 1.0 era.

 

Natural disasters. The tragic and frightening LA fires a reminder of this ongoing theme. The frequency seem to be rising. The costs seem to be rising. Away from the first order impacts…..there are the second order ramifications. It has been shocking to me how many homes are uninsured (or uninsurable) as a result of the rising risks due to fire, flood, wind etc. If we just look at Florida the probabilities of total value loss for property are materially hgher today than they were 10 yeras ago. What used to be viewed as a 1 in 200 yr risk event is now looking more (and priced more) as a 1 in 50 year event. That doesn’t seem to be impacting demand for the property itsefl at this stage…..but….some stats that were interesting to me…..Florida has $400bn to 500bn of property debt….and if you assume a 50 to 70% LTV rate. There is a state subsidised insurance fund that underwrites those loans….and that fund has around $17bn of capital. If we look at Hurricane Milton last year…that caused ~$40bn of damage….and so there is a shortfall. That shortfall will likely be offset with rising insurance costs….and like the cost in london of insurance a first time driver or insuring a range rover….at some point the costs make the asset too hard to purchase. As a secondary note…it is another form of stealth inflation and it does end up (like covid) as a federal/parent problem (deficit). On a different note…prob worth looking at the pricing power of re-insurers and their recent rev growth.

 

Asset management. The asset management industry has moved from intuition and insight towards process. From active to passive. From human to Quant. The path to success seems to have been around scale or being niche, and a more reflexive mindset that trend follows and focusses on asset gathering and product launches rather than a pure focus on mandate and returns. We have seen other structural changes….such as the shift to private and the growth of assets across private equity and private credit (beneficiaries of that duration arbitrage). I did think the news around PE approach for Hargreaves Lansdowne as being interesting. Could PE use that asset base more effectively? Pushing some assets into alts and diversifying their funding and aum base….or/even allocate to the same people that HL currently allocate too but at lower fees and with longer duration. All of which raises the question of whether we are starting to see a trough for both UK assets (relative) and for the active asset management space. More generally we are seeing evolution everywhere….JV’s between active mutual funds and PE….more creative sources of funding and financing to back private equity and private debt activity….the growth of SMA’s and pick and mix allocation to create bespoke bundles of alpha streams. For me… the biggest arbitrages that remain is duration and scale….at LP, GP or investor level. The biggest seem to be benefitting from economies of scale in terms of asset raising and deal winning. The biggest also seem to benefitting from being afforded more time and focus on years rather than months and quarters.

 

 

PRIVATE;

M&A. the stats in 2024 saw a return of activity. Corporate strategic activity accounting for 71% of volumes…..number of $10bn transactions rising by 26% globally….number of $10bn transactions in Europe up by over 40%. A combination of more ‘stability’ driving CEO’s to pivot from defence to offence (especially as GDP tailwinds likely not enough going forward). Concurrent was PE starting to tentatively deploy once more….as the ‘dry powder’ and time into fund life began to burn hoels in pockets. As we head into 2025….corporate simplification, cross border consolidation, Ai and Ai infrastructure as well as leveraging expensive paper and strong FX (US PE and $) as areas and reasons we think could drive more activity. All dependent on Macro environment and rates not messing up the picture!

 

Venture Capital. A question around when and if there is going to be a VC reckoning. Much of the capital deployed in 2020/21 will have been at valuations that companies simply can’t/won’t grow into it. 2023….was an annus horribilis…..a year where Mckinsey highlighted that Capital calls exceeded distributions by 80%.....that VC private secondaries traded at an average discount of 68% of NAV and that fund raising environment was slow and painful with time to fund close…..taking a record high of 20.1 months and first time funds raising $41m for the lowest total since 2013. Deal value in Q1 2024 was 16.3bn….an uptick from Q4 2023 but down more than 50% from Q1 2022 levels. The time inbetween VC rounds has risen to a 10 year high of 1.7 years in Q1 2024, with more capital flowing to the winners with proven business models and margin sustainability. 1 year rolling IRR’s are also at 10 year lows….which in part explains why ‘hold’ times for fund are getting longer with close to 40% of Unicorns having been held for 9 years or more (although this is also due to private for longer too). We’ve seen waves of investment into themes that have fallen out of favour (fast delivery, E-commerce, influencer economy, NFTs). If you look at the big building blocks that captured growth capital ….we saw Covid related remote/digitalisation of everything…only now to be shifting back to a 5 day week office analog world in many spaces. We saw a flurry of Crypto and Nft related startups on to see a few notable falls from grace in the space. We saw a lot of ‘green tech’ and energy transition now dealing  with higher cost of capital and is much greater….LP’s exposure to alts and privates much higher….and the cash return/exit profile having been stretched out longer than many modelled. Then Ai appeared as a white knight…the problem being here that it is still unclear where the terminal value will be created…at the API or LLM layer or the users of the products? Is it a winner takes all game? Who can fund the incredibly capex intensive demands of Ai and Chip growth plays? So time will tell what the return profile of the recent round of Ai Unicorn chasing/creating…but if DPI is the new IRR then it is possible that Ai merely was the cherry on the cake of a final ‘theme’ to chase that provided a suspension of disbelief. And that venture as an asset class, while still as attractive as ever in an era of innovation and tech disruption, will likely have a window of greater dispersion in fund performance and a higher % of below hurdle returns at end of fund life in the short term. 

 

Venture capital part 2. A client asked me if we are entering venture capitals cold winter? Perhaps not for the scale players, or the disciplined funds but for some of the players with less of a track record and with more of a recency bias. Ultimately Venture is captive capital focussed on smaller opportunity set. The problem has been a proliferation of funds, a growth in capital and increased competition. When a decent asset appears – competition huge driving valuations which creates a higher hurdle to meet benchmark. Increasingly a marketing style component of logo ‘wins’ and deal participation with judgement being reserved for the outer years. A shift towards getting access and winning deals rather than returns. Faster deployment and shorter fund raising cycles. All of which leads to a probable decay in returns for many funds. If we do some back of envelope calcs….if you raised fund 1 in 2019….then you deployed in 19/20/21 and then raised fund 2 in 21 and then deployed in 21 and 22….you prob own some digital, some cyber, some web 3.0, some influencer, some e-comm, some social media, some climate tech, some saas and then some ai. Much of which at extremely high valuations. Power law may save you but if you don’t capture one shooting star in each fund…you are in real trouble and that will take several years to wash through as lack of dpi and capital return…turns into crystalised poor performance….turns into harder fund raising. It took many years to get there but valuation discipline now yielding. 

 

Venture capital part 3. A client shared some stats with me….from 2012 to 2021…a combination of a shift early…and public to private as well as ZIRP drove a 8x in terms of capital deployed into US growth ($350bn). 2021’s annual VC investment doubled that of the previous record (2020) with more unicorns created than in the prior five years combined. The number of unicorns being created became so commonplace that the need for a new classification to be created…. ‘decacorns’. As a result….participation from crossover funds and other non-traditional investors reached record highs…as did fund sizes, deployment cycles and fundraising timelines…all of which….drove late-stage valuations to new highs. What we saw in the form of excess liquidity and return chasing in the private markets was mirrored in the public markets. 2021 saw $148b of US IPO issuance and a record 70 VC-backed IPOs – both higher than any prior period in history. Worth noting….retail also was contributing factor to this frenzy…I think to some degree best reflected by the following aberrations….Robotics maker ABB saw call options surge as ‘investors’ mistook ‘ABB’ for ‘ABNB’. ‘Ticker Confusion’ also boosted shares of door manufacturer Masonite (‘DOOR’) ahead of DoorDash’s (‘DASH’) debut.

 

Buybacks. Close to a $1 trillion of authorisations in the US and $300bn in Europe. The Magnificent 7 accounted for 26% of buybacks in the index in 2023, and fourth-quarter filings show they're currently authorized to repurchase $215 billion in stock, 30% higher than the level authorized at the same time last year.) The theme of buybacks as the ‘big buyer’ of the market and a driver of Equity withdrawal has been notable. Given the concentration of the market and the few names that are delivering the majority of the market performance the question around catalysts and momentum for the rest of the market is topical. In many cases the issue is sector and willingness to own (Oil, Miners) for others it might be region (UK) or liquidity (small/midcap). Private equity will view some of this as an opportunity, and the steady stream of take privates from the FTSE 250 is illustrative of that. But…..increasingly…. In the absence of the broader market caring on a nam….you can rely on a company caring. As the Shell Ceo recently said… “I will keep buying back those shares, and buying back those shares, at a discount. That’s a fantastic investment opportunity.” I guess it is all about time frames…the market can ignore you due to theme (ESG) or sector fashion (growth) but ultimately….as the cyclical complex is proving as we speak…as long as a company delivers on plan, buys stock and returns capital….they can keep buying back own shares until they run out of shares to buy or stock gets to a fair valuation.

The need to take risk. Apple spent $20bn on EV cars and then cancelled it. Meta billions on virtual reality then pivoted. In recent weeks it feels like Tesla ‘scrapping’ model 2.0 and Amazon giving up on walk out checkout tech in grocery stores are recent examples of the same theme. The same debate rages around Ai….the capex and the path to revenue creation and return on investment. Right now….for mega cap tech…. the cost of making mistakes vs a mountain of cash on balance sheet has never been smaller….but still worth remembering that mistakes happen. Many (including Steve Ballmer) argued that Apple had missed mobile and couldn’t build a product to compete at a premium price point. A market owned by Motorola, Nokia and Blackberry….that assumed that the business user wanted one thing and that consumers wanted something else…and that a few companies had developed a deep moat and a dominant market share in each segment. At the end of the day..what Apple did was bridge design with engineering as well as bridge a consumer use case (computer and walkman in your pocket) with a business case (no more tiny buttons to fat finger) and effectively took over both. That being said, at the time it was a risk….a risk to not release a flip phone and ‘miss’ mobile…and a risk to release such a high priced product. Risks for growth matter…and along the way mistakes happen (google glass, fire phone, crystal pepsi….to name but a few!) but in the long term….it is these risks that create value.

 

Market behavioural psychology. Maths and physics are guided by facts and laws, finance/markets on the other hand are different. Yes, they are guided by fundamentals and maths…but also by hopes and dreams, sentiment, behaviours and animal spirits. Morgan Housel said it well...”we all think we know how the world works, but we've all only experienced a tiny sliver of it”. Studying history may make you feel like you understand something..but until you've lived through it....experienced the fear and hope...felt its consequences....you may not understand it. Now apply this to current markets. 2023 was year where we all searched for a bear narrative and saw multiple reasons to call a/the top on the market. Many pointed to the extended nature of the cycle and the various reasons why something was amiss (the 14 year nature of the bull market, the lack of bankruptcies, the multiples relative to history etc). Perhaps this is a function of a generational bias and the mindset of those who saw and experienced 2000/01 or 2008/09. If you look back through time you see other examples of this. If you were born in the 1950's and 'experienced' equity markets for the next 15/25 years....you generated close to zero returns adjusted for inflation...on the other hand...if you were born in the 1970's the next 20/30 years delivered you close to a 10x. Each generation has a different experience and their future behaviour is going to be biased by that experience. 

 

PE vintages.  If we had to sum up private markets through 2023 in three words, they'd be: "lack of distributions." Global exit activity remained at muted levels throughout last year—and so far in 2024—as GPs wait for decreasing interest rates and a reopening of the IPO window. Cost of capital and uncertainty of how rates/inflation will impact exit multiples has resulted in caution being applied. While PE vintages 2016, 2017, 2018, and 2021 are following within the range of J-curve patterns set by the 2006-to-2013 cohort…..many LPs who invested in large (and fast deployed) 2019 and 2020 funds have seen steep negative cash flows. Digging deeper….the 2019 and 2020 vintages have been hit by the triple whammy of the pandemic wiping out years 1 and 2 early in their fund lives……followed by many funds seeing years 3 and 4 spent chasing the market and buying companies at elevated ever valuations…followed by the interest-rate-hike cycle during their respective fourth and fifth years, which has tested the validity of those valuations vs public listed peers.
